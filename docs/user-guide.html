Family Call Center: User Guide

A kids' landline that lets them call one number, leave you a secret, or hear the weather. Written to be read cold, with no prior knowledge of the app assumed.

1. What this is

This is the little phone system behind a kid's landline. The child has a simple corded phone (the Tin Can is the one this was built around), and it dials one number. That number rings this app, which answers and plays a short menu: press 1 to leave a voicemail, press 2 to hear the weather.

It runs on a Raspberry Pi at home. When a voicemail comes in, the Pi saves the recording, deletes it from the phone company so the Pi holds the only copy, and sends you a push notification on your phone. You listen back from a small private web page that only works on your home network.

What it is not: it is not the dialing service for the kid's phone itself, and it does not replace a real phone line. It is just the friendly robot that answers when the kid calls this one number. It does not transcribe messages to text, the weather is United States only, and the only person who gets notified is whoever you point the notifications at.

The rest of this guide walks the two things a kid does (press 1, press 2), the two things you get (a notification, an inbox), where the files live, and how it is wired up.

2. Words you need first

IVR
Interactive Voice Response. The "press 1 for this, press 2 for that" menu you hear when you call a business. This app is a tiny, friendly one built for a family.
Twilio
The phone company in the cloud. It owns the actual phone number, answers the call, plays the audio, and records the voicemail. Your Pi just tells Twilio what to do, one step at a time.
The Pi
A Raspberry Pi (a small always-on computer) sitting at your house running this app. It is the brain: it decides what the caller hears, stores the recordings, and sends notifications.
Tailscale Funnel
A way to give your home Pi a public web address so Twilio can reach it over the internet, without opening your home network to the world. Twilio talks to the Pi through this funnel.
Tailnet and MagicDNS
Your private Tailscale network and the friendly hostname for the Pi on it (something like pi.your-tailnet.ts.net). Devices on your tailnet (your phone, your laptop) can reach the Pi privately. The public internet cannot.
The Host gate
The rule that keeps the inbox private. The Pi answers on two addresses (the public funnel and the private tailnet name), and it only serves the inbox and recordings when the request arrives on the private name. Everything else gets a "not found".
Pushover
A simple phone-notification service. The Pi sends it a short message and your phone buzzes. This is how you find out a voicemail was left.
NWS
The United States National Weather Service. Its free forecast feed is what "press 2" reads aloud. No account or API key needed, which is why it was chosen.

3. Calling: the menu

When the kid picks up their phone and calls the number, the app answers and says:

"Welcome. Press 1 to leave a voicemail, or press 2 for the weather."

That is the whole menu. Press 1 and it starts recording. Press 2 and it reads the weather. Press anything else, or wait too long, and it just repeats the menu rather than hanging up, so a kid mashing buttons never gets stuck.

4. Press 1: leave a voicemail

This is the heart of it, the "press 1 to leave mom a secret" part. After the kid presses 1, the app says:

"Please leave your voicemail after the beep. Press pound when you are finished."

Then it beeps and records. A few things are fixed in the code and worth knowing:

  • The message can run up to 5 minutes (300 seconds). After that it stops on its own.
  • Pressing # (pound) ends the recording early. A kid does not have to press anything though; hanging up or going quiet also ends it.
  • When it is done the app says "Thank you. Your message has been saved. Goodbye." and hangs up.

Behind the scenes, the moment the recording finishes the Pi downloads the audio, files it away, deletes it from Twilio, and sends you a notification. The kid does not see any of that. They just leave their secret and hang up.

5. Press 2: hear the weather

Press 2 and the app reads today's weather out loud, in plain words, something like "In [your town], this afternoon will be sunny, with a temperature around 72 degrees. Tonight, clear, around 58 degrees." Then it returns to the menu so the kid can do something else.

The forecast comes from the National Weather Service, which is free and needs no account. You tell the app where "home" is once (a latitude, a longitude, and a spoken place name), and it reads the forecast for that spot.

If the weather service is slow or down, the app does not leave the kid hanging on a dead line. It says "Sorry, I can't get the weather right now. Please try again later," and goes back to the menu. The same thing happens if you have not set a location yet.

6. What you get notified about

You get a Pushover notification on your phone for both menu choices. They are deliberately short:

WhenTitleMessage
A voicemail is leftNew voicemail"[caller] left a [N] second message." Plus a "Listen in the inbox" link that opens the recording on your tailnet.
Someone checks the weatherWeather check"Someone checked the weather from [caller]." A little heads-up that a kid is playing with the phone.

The notifications are best-effort by design. If Pushover is down, or you have not set it up yet, the recording is still saved safely and the caller's experience is unchanged. A failed notification never breaks a voicemail. It just means you missed the buzz, and the message is still waiting in the inbox.

Pushover notifications carry images, not audio, so the message itself is not attached. The "Listen" link is how you actually hear it, which brings us to the inbox.

7. The inbox: listening back

The inbox is a small web page that lists every voicemail newest-first, each with a play button right there in the row. You open it at http://<your-pi-tailnet-name>:8080/ from your phone or laptop.

The important part is privacy. The same Pi answers the public phone traffic and serves this private inbox, but the inbox only works when you reach the Pi by its private tailnet name. If anyone tries to reach the inbox or a recording over the public phone address, they get a plain "not found". Your kids' messages never sit on a public web page.

8. Where a voicemail goes

When a recording finishes, it takes a short journey and ends up living only on your Pi. The app downloads the audio from Twilio, saves it into a dated folder, writes a row into a small database so the inbox can list it, and then deletes the recording from Twilio. That last step matters: it keeps your costs near zero, and it means the Pi holds the only copy.

The Pi is the only copy. Recordings live in data/recordings/ and the list lives in data/ivr.db on the Pi's disk. If that disk dies, the messages are gone. If these recordings matter to you, back up the data/ folder somewhere.

9. Setting it up (for the grown-up)

This part is for whoever runs the Pi, not the kids. The full step-by-step lives in SETUP.md in the project. The short version is four things:

  1. Twilio: buy a phone number and point its voice webhook at your Pi's public address followed by /call.
  2. Fill in .env: your Twilio credentials, your public address, your Pi's private tailnet name, your Pushover keys, and your home weather location. Nothing about your location lives in the code; it all comes from this file.
  3. Tailscale: a public funnel so Twilio can reach the Pi, and the private tailnet name for the inbox.
  4. Run it as a service so it starts on boot and restarts if it crashes.
One setting catches people out: the service must run with HOST=0.0.0.0 (the included service file sets this for you). Without it the app only listens to itself and the inbox will not open from your phone, even on the tailnet. The Host gate still keeps it private.

The settings you provide

SettingWhat it is for
TWILIO_*Your Twilio account and phone number.
BASE_URLThe public funnel address Twilio calls.
TAILNET_HOSTNAMEThe Pi's private name. The inbox only serves requests that arrive on this.
PUSHOVER_TOKEN, PUSHOVER_USERWhere your notifications go. Leave blank and notifications are simply skipped.
WEATHER_LAT, WEATHER_LON, WEATHER_PLACE_NAMEYour home location and its spoken name for "press 2". Leave blank and press 2 politely says it cannot get the weather.

10. Good to know

Can other people on the internet hear my kid's voicemails?
No. The inbox and the recordings are only served to devices on your private tailnet. A request to the same address from the public side gets a plain "not found", not the audio.
What happens if the Pi is off or the internet is down?
Twilio cannot reach the app, so the caller will not get the menu. Nothing is lost; there is just no answer until the Pi is back. Once it is back, new calls work again.
What if I have not set up notifications or weather yet?
Both are optional and fail gracefully. With Pushover unset, voicemails are still recorded and saved, you just do not get a buzz. With no weather location, press 2 says it cannot get the weather and returns to the menu.
Does it cost anything?
Twilio charges a small monthly fee for the phone number plus per-minute charges for calls and recordings. The app deletes each recording from Twilio right after saving it, so storage cost stays near zero. The weather feed and Pushover are inexpensive or free.
Why a kid's phone and not just a smartphone app?
That is the whole point. The kid uses a simple corded phone with no screen and no apps. This system is what gives that one phone something fun and useful to do.
Is there a "press 3"?
Not in this version. The menu is press 1 (voicemail) and press 2 (weather). Anything else just repeats the menu. More options can be added later the same way press 2 was.
How long can a message be?
Up to five minutes. The child can end it sooner by pressing pound or just hanging up.
