Family Call Center: User Guide

A kids' landline that lets them call one number, leave a message for you, or hear today's weather. Written to be read cold, with no prior knowledge of the app assumed.

1. What this is

This is the little phone system behind a kid's landline. The child has a simple corded phone (the Tin Can is the one this was built around), and it dials one number. That number rings this app, which answers in a warm, friendly voice and plays a short menu: press 1 to leave a message, press 2 to hear the weather.

It runs on a Raspberry Pi at home. The whole call speaks in a warm Deepgram Aura voice, slowed down a little so a child can follow along. When a message comes in, the Pi saves the recording, transcribes it to text, deletes it from the phone company so the Pi holds the only copy, and sends you a push notification with the transcript right there in the ping. You listen back from a small private web page that only works on your home network.

What it is not: it does not let the child dial out to other numbers, and it is not a replacement for a real phone line. It is just the friendly robot that answers when the kid calls this one number. The weather works anywhere in the world (Open-Meteo covers global forecasts). The only person who gets notified is whoever you point the notifications at.

The rest of this guide walks the two things a kid does (press 1, press 2), how the voice works, the two things you get (a notification, an inbox), where the files live, and how it is wired up.

2. Words you need first

IVR
Interactive Voice Response. The "press 1 for this, press 2 for that" menu you hear when you call a business. This app is a tiny, friendly one built for a family.
Twilio
The phone company in the cloud. It owns the actual phone number, answers the call, plays the audio, and records the message. Your Pi just tells Twilio what to do, one step at a time.
The Pi
A Raspberry Pi (a small always-on computer) sitting at your house running this app. It is the brain: it decides what the caller hears, stores the recordings, and sends notifications.
Deepgram Aura (the voice)
The service that turns written text into the warm spoken voice you hear on the call. The deployed voice is "luna" (model aura-2-luna-en), slowed to 85% speed by ffmpeg so a child can follow it easily. Every prompt is synthesized once and cached on the Pi, so there is no delay during the call.
Deepgram nova-3 (transcription)
The same service, but in reverse: it listens to the recorded message and turns it into text. That text goes straight into your Pushover notification so you can read the message without even opening the inbox.
Open-Meteo
A free, open weather API. No account or API key needed. The app uses it to pull today's forecast for your home location once every morning at 4 a.m. and cache it for the day.
Pushover
A simple phone-notification service. The Pi sends it a short message and your phone buzzes. This is how you find out a message was left, and you get the transcript right in the ping.
Cloudflare tunnel
A named tunnel that gives the home Pi a stable public web address so Twilio can reach it, without opening the home network; the address stays the same across reboots.
Tailnet and MagicDNS
Your private Tailscale network and the friendly hostname for the Pi on it (something like pi.your-tailnet.ts.net). Devices on your tailnet (your phone, your laptop) can reach the Pi privately. The public internet cannot.
The Host gate
The rule that keeps the inbox private. The Pi answers on two addresses (the public Cloudflare address and the private tailnet name), and it only serves the inbox and recordings when the request arrives on the private name. Everything else gets a plain "not found".

3. Calling: the menu

When the kid picks up their phone and calls the number, the app answers and says:

"Hi there! So glad you called. Press 1 to leave a message. Or press 2 to hear today's weather!"

That is the whole menu. Press 1 and it starts recording. Press 2 and it reads the weather. Press anything else, or wait too long without pressing anything, and it says "Oops, I didn't catch that. Press 1 to leave a message. Or press 2 for the weather!" and tries again rather than hanging up, so a kid mashing buttons never gets stuck.

4. Press 1: leave a message

This is the heart of it: the "press 1 to leave mom a secret" part. After the kid presses 1, the app says:

"Okay! After the beep, tell me your message. When you're all done, press the pound key, or just hang up."

Then it beeps and records. A few things are fixed in the code and worth knowing:

  • The message can run up to 5 minutes (300 seconds). After that it stops on its own.
  • Pressing # (pound) ends the recording early. The kid does not have to press anything though; hanging up also ends it.
  • When it is done, the app says "Got it! Your message is saved. Talk to you soon. Bye bye!" and hangs up.

Behind the scenes, once the recording finishes the Pi downloads the audio, sends it to Deepgram to transcribe, saves the audio file, deletes it from Twilio, and sends you a Pushover notification with the transcript right there in the message. The kid does not see any of that. They just leave their secret and hang up.

If transcription succeeds, the notification shows the transcript in quotes. If Deepgram heard nothing, it says "(No words were heard in this message.)". If transcription failed for any reason, it says "(Transcription failed.)". It never hides the failure or pretends it worked.

5. Press 2: hear the weather

Press 2 and the app reads today's weather out loud, in plain, friendly words. It opens with the date, throws in a goofy weather line, then gives the outfit advice and any reminders, then both the morning and afternoon temperatures. Here is what a real forecast sounds like on a rainy school-day morning:

"Good morning! It's Tuesday, June 24th. Drip, drop! It's a splishy-splashy puddle day. Grab your rain boots! Today is a pants and long sleeve day. And bring your rain jacket! This morning it's about 58 degrees. And this afternoon, it'll be about 65 degrees and rainy."

The app plays that forecast twice in a row (so the child can catch anything they missed), then says "Have a great day! Bye bye!" and hangs up.

How the forecast line is built

The spoken line has a fixed shape:

  1. Date: always opens with "Good morning! It's [day of week], [Month] [date]."
  2. Goofy line: a fun one-liner picked for the weather type (sunny, cloudy, rainy, snowy, stormy, foggy). It rotates by date so it is never the same two days in a row.
  3. Outfit: "Today is [outfit description]." On school days (Monday through Friday) the bands are slightly more conservative than on weekends. Both use the day's high temperature to pick the right outfit tier.
  4. Reminders: automatically added when conditions call for them: a jacket reminder if the morning is cold or warms up a lot by afternoon, a sunscreen reminder if UV is high, and a rain jacket reminder if there is 40% or higher chance of rain.
  5. Temperatures: closes with the morning high (8 a.m. to noon) and the afternoon high (noon to 4 p.m.) in degrees Fahrenheit.

All of this (the outfit bands, the goofy lines for each weather type, the temperature thresholds for reminders, the school-vs-weekend distinction, and individual date overrides like holidays) lives in config/wardrobe.yml and config/day_overrides.yml. They are plain text files you can edit freely without touching any code.

The forecast data comes from Open-Meteo, a free global weather API with no account or API key required. The Pi fetches it fresh once a day at 4 a.m. and caches it so the call is instant. If the weather service is slow or unreachable when the child calls, the app says "Hmm, I can't check the weather right now. Try me again in a little while!" and hangs up.

You also get a Pushover notification showing exactly what the child heard, so you know both that they called and what the forecast said.

6. The voice

Every word the child hears is spoken by a Deepgram Aura neural voice (currently "luna", model aura-2-luna-en). The audio is generated at normal speed by Deepgram, then slowed to 85% using ffmpeg's atempo filter (pitch is preserved; just the pace is easier to follow for a young child).

Every fixed prompt (the menu greeting, the voicemail prompt, the thank-you, the weather goodbye, and the fallback messages) is pre-generated at startup and cached on disk. During a live call, the app never waits on Deepgram; it just plays the cached file. The weather forecast text is also synthesized and cached right after the 4 a.m. weather refresh.

If Deepgram is unavailable or not configured, the app falls back to Twilio's plain built-in voice (<Say>) so the call always works. The child might notice a different voice, but they will not get silence or an error.

7. What you get notified about

You get a Pushover notification on your phone for both menu choices. They arrive right after the call finishes each step.

WhenTitleWhat the message contains
A message is left New voicemail The caller's number, the duration in seconds, and the transcript of what they said (or a note if transcription was empty or failed). Includes a "Listen in the inbox" link to your tailnet inbox if you have a tailnet hostname configured.
Someone checks the weather Weather check The caller's number followed by the full text the child heard (the same forecast that was read aloud). A little heads-up that a kid is using the phone, plus the exact forecast so you know what they were told.

Both notifications are best-effort. If Pushover is down, or you have not set it up yet, the recording is still saved and the caller's experience is completely unchanged. A failed notification never breaks a call or a recording.

8. The inbox: listening back

The inbox is a small web page that lists every message newest-first, each with a play button right there in the row. You open it at http://<your-pi-tailnet-name>:8080/ from your phone or laptop.

The important part is privacy. The same Pi answers the public phone traffic and serves this private inbox, but the inbox only works when you reach the Pi by its private tailnet name. If anyone tries to reach the inbox or a recording over the public web address, they get a plain "not found": not a login page, not an error, just nothing. Your kids' messages never sit on a public web page.

9. Where things live / privacy

When a recording finishes, it takes a short journey and ends up living only on your Pi. The app downloads the audio from Twilio, saves it into a dated folder under data/recordings/, writes a row into a small SQLite database (data/ivr.db) so the inbox can list it, and then deletes the recording from Twilio. That last step matters: it keeps your costs near zero, and it means the Pi holds the only copy.

The Pi is the only copy. If the Pi's disk dies, the recordings are gone. If these messages matter to you, back up the data/ folder to somewhere else.

A few other things worth knowing about what the system does and does not store:

  • Your home location (latitude and longitude) lives only in the .env file on the Pi. It is never in the application code, never sent to Twilio, and never included in any notification.
  • Recordings are deleted from Twilio immediately after the Pi downloads them. Twilio holds the audio for only a few seconds.
  • The inbox is invisible to the public internet. There is no login page or access control to bypass; the inbox simply does not exist from the public side.

10. Setting it up (for the grown-up)

This part is for whoever runs the Pi. The full durable Raspberry Pi setup lives in DEPLOY.md; SETUP.md covers a quick laptop test. The short version is the pieces you need in place:

  1. Twilio: buy a phone number and point its voice webhook at your Pi's public web address followed by /call.
  2. Deepgram: a free or paid Deepgram account for the voice and transcription. Without it the app falls back to Twilio's built-in voice and skips transcription, so it still works, just without the warm voice and in-notification transcript.
  3. Pushover: optional but recommended. Create an app token and a user key; add them to .env. Without them, everything still records; you just do not get a ping.
  4. Cloudflare: a named tunnel giving the Pi a stable public address for Twilio, unchanged across restarts.
  5. Tailscale: the private tailnet that serves the inbox to your own devices only.
  6. ffmpeg: installed on the Pi for the speech-rate adjustment. Without it the voice plays at normal speed instead of the slowed-down rate.
One setting catches people out: the service must run with HOST=0.0.0.0 (the included service file sets this for you). Without it the app only listens to itself and the inbox will not open from your phone, even on the tailnet. The Host gate still keeps it private.

The settings you provide in .env

SettingWhat it is for
TWILIO_*Your Twilio account credentials and phone number.
BASE_URLThe stable public address (a named Cloudflare tunnel) Twilio calls back to.
TAILNET_HOSTNAMEThe Pi's private tailnet name. The inbox only serves requests that arrive on this hostname.
DEEPGRAM_API_KEYYour Deepgram key for voice synthesis and transcription. Leave blank and the app falls back to Twilio's built-in voice with no transcription.
DEEPGRAM_TTS_MODELWhich Aura voice to use. Defaults to aura-2-andromeda-en; the deployed voice is aura-2-luna-en.
SPEECH_RATEPlayback speed for synthesized audio. 1.0 is normal; 0.85 is the deployed value (slowed for a child). Requires ffmpeg when not 1.0.
PUSHOVER_TOKEN, PUSHOVER_USERWhere your notifications go. Leave blank and notifications are simply skipped.
WEATHER_LAT, WEATHER_LON, WEATHER_PLACE_NAMEYour home coordinates and the spoken place name for the forecast. Leave blank and press 2 politely says it cannot get the weather.

11. Good to know

Can other people on the internet hear my kid's messages?
No. The inbox and the recordings are only served to devices on your private tailnet. A request from the public side gets a plain "not found": not a login form, not the audio. There is nothing to bypass.
Does it come back on its own after a reboot or power cut?
Yes. The app and the Cloudflare tunnel both run as systemd services that start on boot. After any reboot or power cut, the Pi comes back up, the services restart automatically, and the line answers again with no intervention from you.
What happens if the Pi is off or the internet is down?
Twilio cannot reach the app, so the caller will not get the menu. Nothing is lost; there is just no answer until the Pi is back. Once it is back, new calls work normally.
Does it cost anything?
Twilio charges a small monthly fee for the phone number and per-minute charges for calls. The app deletes each recording from Twilio right after saving it, so storage costs stay near zero. Open-Meteo is free with no account required. Deepgram has a free tier. Pushover is a one-time small purchase.
Can I change the voice, the speed, or the jokes?
Yes to all three. The voice and speed are set by DEEPGRAM_TTS_MODEL and SPEECH_RATE in your .env file. The goofy weather lines and outfit advice live in config/wardrobe.yml, a plain text file you can edit freely. After changing the voice or speed, restart the app so it re-synthesizes the cached prompts.
How does it know whether today is a school day?
Monday through Friday are school days by default; Saturday and Sunday are weekend days. You can override any individual date (for holidays, days off, or anything else) in config/day_overrides.yml. School-day and weekend outfit bands are slightly different; the weekend bands are a little more casual.
Is there a "press 3"?
Not in this version. The menu is press 1 (message) and press 2 (weather). Anything else just repeats the menu. More options can be added the same way press 2 was.
How long can a message be?
Up to five minutes. The child can end it sooner by pressing pound or just hanging up.
What if transcription fails?
The recording is still saved. The Pushover notification will say "(Transcription failed.)" so you know, and you can still listen to the full audio in the inbox. The failure is always reported honestly.
What if the Pi is running but Deepgram is down?
The app falls back to Twilio's built-in text-to-speech for that call. The child will hear a slightly different, less warm voice, but the call will work and the recording will still be saved. Transcription of that message will say "(Transcription failed.)" in your notification.
When does the weather refresh?
At 4 a.m. every day (set in config/wardrobe.yml under schedule). It also refreshes once at startup when the app boots, so a freshly restarted Pi does not serve stale weather. If the child calls between midnight and 4 a.m., they will hear yesterday's cached forecast.
